Part 01 · The task

Turn one sequence into another.

The paper's arena is sequence transduction: map a variable-length input sequence to a variable-length output sequence, where the alignment between the two is latent — nobody tells the model which output token corresponds to which input token.

Machine translation

Machine translation is the headline act — it's what the paper benchmarks — but the same formulation covers parsing (the paper's own generalization test: English → constituency trees), summarization, speech-to-text, and later code, proteins, and images. Language modeling is the degenerate case where input and output are the same sequence, shifted by one. Hold that thought for the next talk.

Part 02 · Life before

Recurrence was the law, and the law was slow.

In 2017 the state of the art — LSTMs and GRUs — read text the way we do: one token at a time, left to right, squeezing everything seen so far into a fixed-size hidden state. Elegant. And crippling, for two separate reasons.

The parallelism problem

Step t needs the hidden state of step t−1. Within one training example, a recurrent net cannot be parallelized — no matter how many chips you own.

RNN / LSTM
t = 0
Transformer
t = 0
RNN

One state to rule them all

The entire past is compressed into one vector. Gradients flowing back through hundreds of steps vanish or explode — long-range dependencies dissolve.

LSTM / GRU

Gates buy time, not freedom

Gating (1997!) lets information survive longer, and attention bolted on top helped. But computation is still a chain: O(n) sequential steps, memory-bound, slow to train.

ConvS2S · ByteNet

Convolutions: parallel, but myopic

CNN stacks compute in parallel, yet relating position 1 to position n takes O(n/k) or O(log n) stacked layers. Distance still costs depth.

Bonus aside for the graph-minded: a Transformer is essentially a message-passing GNN running on the complete graph over tokens — every node talks to every node, edge weights computed on the fly.

Part 03 · Why Google, why then

Translate was drowning in its own success.

Google Translate served 103 languages and over 100 billion words a day. The 2016 neural system (GNMT) beat phrase-based translation — at brutal cost: LSTM stacks trained for days on ~100 GPUs, per direction. Now do the math on directions.

552

directed translation pairs · n(n−1)

Pairs grow quadratically. At 103 languages that's 10,506 directions — you cannot train, store, or serve one bespoke model per pair. You need shared, general language representations. And a model cheap enough to retrain constantly.

…and the data is wildly lopsided

Parallel corpora exist where politics and commerce created them. The paper's own two benchmarks differ by 8×; most of the world's pairs sit near zero.

So the institutional pressure at Google in 2016–17: a translation architecture that trains fast enough to iterate, parallelizes across cheap accelerators, and generalizes across languages instead of memorizing one pair. That is the shape of the thing they went looking for.

Part 04 · Standing on shoulders

Not one idea in it was new.

Every ingredient already existed. The paper's contribution was a subtraction: keep three inherited ideas, delete the recurrence they were welded to.

Inherited · 2014

Encoder–decoder

Sutskever et al.'s seq2seq: one network reads the source into representations, another writes the target. The Transformer keeps this two-tower blueprint exactly.

Inherited · 2014–15

Attention as alignment

Bahdanau et al. let the decoder "look back" at all encoder states with learned soft weights — killing the fixed-vector bottleneck. Attention was born as an add-on to RNNs.

Inherited · forever

Autoregressive factorization

Generate y one token at a time, each conditioned on everything before: p(y)=∏ p(yᵢ|y<ᵢ, x). The Transformer doesn't touch this — remember that for Part 08.

The title is a thesis statement, not a slogan. The question the paper actually asks: if attention is doing the useful work inside recurrent translation models, do we need the recurrence at all?

Part 05 · What was actually new

Every token looks at every token. At once.

Self-attention: each position computes what to pull from every other position — in one matrix multiply, no steps, no chain. Distance costs nothing. Here is the paper's own example (its Figure 8), live:

“it” attends to animal — the model resolves the pronoun. Now swap the adjective and watch the same word change its mind.

The mechanism
Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dk) V
Q · query

What this token is looking for. “I'm a pronoun — who's my referent?”

K · key

What each token advertises. Query·key dot products become compatibility scores.

V · value

What a token hands over once selected. The output is a weighted average of values.

√d̄ₖ · the scale

Dot products grow with dimension; unscaled, softmax saturates and gradients die. One tiny division fixes it.

Run that h = 8 times in parallel with different learned projections (d_k = d_v = 64 each, concatenated back to 512) and you get multi-head attention — eight subspaces attending to different things, like the three heads you toggled above. Attention is used three ways in the model: encoder self-attention, masked decoder self-attention, and decoder→encoder cross-attention.

One problem: attention is a bag, not a sequence

Delete recurrence and word order vanishes — “dog bites man” = “man bites dog”. The fix: add position into the embedding itself, as sinusoids of geometrically spaced frequencies. Every position gets a unique waveform fingerprint; relative offsets become linear transforms.

Drag the slider: the vertical stripe is the “barcode” a token at that position carries into the model.

The full machine
step 1 / 6

Tokens become vectors

Byte-pair encoding splits text into ~37k subword units — rare words become composable pieces (no unknown-word problem across languages). Each unit looks up a 512-dim embedding.

step 2 / 6

Position is injected

The sinusoidal barcode is simply added to each embedding. This is the only place the model is told about order. Everything downstream is permutation machinery plus this hint.

step 3 / 6

The encoder: 6 identical layers

Each layer: multi-head self-attention (every source token consults all others), then a position-wise feed-forward net (512→2048→512). Residual connections and LayerNorm around both — the plumbing that lets it train deep.

step 4 / 6

The decoder attends to itself — masked

Target tokens self-attend, but a causal mask blanks out the future. The quiet trick of the whole paper: training on all positions simultaneously while each still only sees its past. Autoregressive semantics, parallel compute.

step 5 / 6

Cross-attention: where translation happens

Queries come from the target side; keys and values from the encoder output. This is Bahdanau's alignment reborn — the decoder interrogating the source at every layer.

step 6 / 6

Linear → softmax → next token

A distribution over the vocabulary; at inference, beam search (beam 4) walks it token by token. Output embedding weights are shared with the input table. 65M parameters total, base config.

Optimization

Warm up, then decay

Adam (β₂=0.98) with the now-famous schedule: linear warmup for 4,000 steps, then inverse-square-root decay. Plus dropout 0.1 and label smoothing 0.1 — which hurts perplexity but improves BLEU. They optimized the metric that mattered.

Batching

What went in

Batches of ~25,000 source + 25,000 target tokens, sentences bucketed by length. EN–FR used 36M pairs with a 32k word-piece vocab. Final models average the last few checkpoints — no exotic tricks, everything reproducible on one box.

Total training compute, big model: 2.3 × 10¹⁹ FLOPs. Remember that number.

Part 07 · Blast radius

A translation paper ate the whole field.

Roughly 200,000 citations later, the Transformer is less an architecture than a substrate. The pattern below repeats: someone takes the 2017 blueprint, deletes half of it, points it at a new modality, and it wins.

2017

Transformer — and, same year, sparse MoE

Noam Shazeer co-authors both this paper and “Outrageously Large Neural Networks” (mixture-of-experts). The two threads braid together for a decade — see Part 08.

2018

BERT: keep only the encoder

Bidirectional pre-training + fine-tuning sweeps every NLP benchmark and lands inside Google Search. First proof that the blueprint transfers beyond translation. (GPT-1 quietly keeps only the decoder…)

2019

GPT-2 — deliberately skipped

Decoder-only scaling, language modeling as the universal task: that story belongs to the next talk in this seminar. No spoilers here.

2020

ViT: an image is worth 16×16 words

Chop an image into patches, call them tokens, feed the plain 2017 encoder. CNNs' decade of dominance in vision ends. Scaling laws (Kaplan et al.) make “just make it bigger” a research program.

2021

AlphaFold 2: attention over proteins

Attention over residue pairs cracks protein structure prediction — later a Nobel Prize in Chemistry. The clearest sign this is a general computation, not a language trick.

2022

Whisper, FlashAttention, and the chat moment

Speech recognition falls to an off-the-shelf encoder–decoder Transformer (Whisper). FlashAttention makes exact attention IO-efficient. And a certain chat product makes the architecture a household topic.

2023–26

The substrate era

Open weights (LLaMA and descendants), multimodal models, code assistants, reasoning models — plus the first credible challengers (Mamba, diffusion LMs) which, tellingly, still keep some attention layers. Onward to the bills.

Part 08 · The bills attention left

Every token attends to every token. That's the bug now.

The paper solved training-time parallelism and left two time bombs: O(n²) attention cost as contexts grew from 70 tokens to a million, and — inherited untouched from Part 04 — one-token-at-a-time autoregressive generation. Most of the last five years of systems research is paying these two debts.

Debt №1 · inference memory

The KV cache: attention's rent, in gigabytes

To avoid recomputing the past at every generated token, you cache every layer's keys and values — trading the quadratic recompute for a memory bill that grows with every token of context. For a 70B-class model:

40 GB of cache — before weights
H100 · 80 GB
B200 · 192 GB

fix · cache smaller

MQA → GQA → MLA

Share keys/values across query heads (MQA/GQA, 8× smaller), or project them into a compressed latent (DeepSeek's MLA, ~30× smaller) — toggle them above. Same attention, thriftier rent.

fix · move bytes smarter

FlashAttention

Attention was never compute-bound — it was drowning in memory traffic. Tile the computation to stay in on-chip SRAM: exact same math, 2–4× faster, and long contexts become trainable at all.

fix · attend less

Sparse attention

Sliding windows, global tokens (Longformer/BigBird), and learned block selection (NSA, MoBA — 2025) cut n² to near-linear by betting most token pairs never mattered.

fix · change the math

Linear attention & SSMs

Kernelized attention, then Mamba's state-space models: O(n), constant-memory inference — recurrence, reborn with better parenting. Production models now ship as hybrids: mostly Mamba, a few attention layers.

fix · guess then check

Speculative decoding

A small drafter proposes k tokens; the big model verifies them in one parallel pass — accept on agreement, provably identical output, 2–3× faster. The 2017 parallelism trick, smuggled into generation.

fix · pay per token

Mixture of experts

Shazeer's other 2017 idea, industrialized: hundreds of expert FFNs, a router activates a few per token. Parameters decouple from FLOPs — trillion-parameter capacity at billion-parameter cost.

Debt №2 · autoregression itself — a 2026 exhibit

NVIDIA Nemotron “TwoTower” 30B-A3B

This model attacks exactly that: a frozen autoregressive context tower reads the prompt, and a diffusion denoiser tower generates whole blocks of tokens in parallel — iteratively unmasking them with bidirectional attention inside each block. Part 09 · Coda

Attention was all we needed — to get started.

Nine years on, the 2017 blueprint is being disassembled by the same logic that created it: find the part doing the real work, delete the rest. It happened to recurrence. It's happening to full attention, to dense FFNs, and now to autoregression itself. That's not the paper failing — that's the paper's method, still running.
